The Treasure Chest

The Biggest Little Toy Shop Online

The Challenge

As a brick-and-mortar store, The Treasure Chest has delighting locals for over 30 years by connecting customers to their childhood with a curated mix of new and vintage toys, games, and magic.

How can the magical, authentic interactions customers have with the brand in-store be translated into an e-commerce setting?

Process and Insights

User card sorting, competitive and comparative analysis, and user research provided the following insights:

  • There are many mental models for shopping for toys, games, and magic.
  • Providing social proof makes for confidant shopping.
  • A holistic approach to designing the online experience that includes both shopping for toys and playing with the toys can meet user needs and business goals.

The Solution

The Treasure Chest online experience helps customers find the right toy for their child through detailed reviews and curated collections, and helps them create memories through getting the most out of their toy with ideas for play.

Desktop Prototype

Duration: 2 Weeks

Role: Solo UX Designer

Toolkit: stakeholder interview, comparative and competitive analysis, card-sorting, information architecture, sketching, storyboarding, user scenarios, user flows, wireframes, paper and digital prototyping, user testing

Deliverables: low-fidelity prototype, presentation to client


Context and Challenge

The Treasure Chest is a fictional store in Utah that aims to create and recreate the magic and treasure of childhood through new and vintage toys, games, and magic tricks. For over 30 years the store has sold its highly-curated inventory and they are looking to bring their store online. Based on the project brief and stakeholder interviews with the business leaders, I set out to meet these goals:

Increase engagement with current customers.

Increase customer acquisition locally and globally.

Translate the in-store experience into a digital experience.

Research and Strategy

My research focused on understanding the landscape for online toy stores, the mental models of people shopping for toys, and the design patterns for "social proof" and "product discovery" experiences. I dug deep into the goals of Jason who shops for his daughter:

  • make informed decisions about what he is buying — both in terms of match to his daughter's interests as well as the type and quality of play experience the toy offers
  • grow relationship through gift-giving and spending time playing together
  • find a toy and give it to his daughter without worrying about all the steps in between

User Research
The client provided 3 well-researched personas: Jason (the single father), Jenny (the grandmother), and Daniel (the collector). I narrowed in on Jason as the primary persona.

Competitive and Comparative Analysis
Jason's goals guided an analysis of other online toy stores, places where people discover unique products, and places that provide strong social proof for their products.

User Card Sorting
I used a list of the store’s top 100 items to conduct an open and closed card sort to better understand mental models for organizing toys (beyond gender and age categories.)

Design Synthesis and Ideation

These research insights evolved into 4 key experiences in Jason's shopping journey:

Find a toy. Jason finds a toy through easy search or curated collections.

Make sure it is "cool." Jason feels confident his daughter will like the toy because the reviews show another child with her similar interests liked it.

Buy and give the toy. Purchase to delivery to gift-giving is transparent and worry-free.

Spend time together playing (and re-engage with the website). Jason can revisit the website to get additional ideas for how to play with the toy with his daughter.


Storyboard, Scenarios, and User Flows
I developed storyboards, scenarios, and flows of Jason's shopping journey from finding a toy to spending time with his daughter playing.

Site Mapping and Navigation
The site map and navigation was informed by an understanding of the user flows and the insights from card-sorting research.

Layout and UI
I sketched many possibe interfaces and conducted user walkthroughs to iterate and narrow in on the key experiences.

User-Tested Solution

The Treasure Chest online experience helps customers find the right toy for their child through detailed reviews and curated collections, and helps them create memories through getting the most out of their toy with ideas for play.

Next steps:

  • Further test current prototype, particularly around the “Inspiration for Play” interaction.
  • Do deeper research for the personas of Jenny and Daniel and solve for more of their needs.
  • Consider developing and exploring a persona for the child using the toys and using this to inform the online toy purchasing experience

Desktop Prototype

Find a toy and make sure it is cool. By shopping at Treasure Chest, Jason can be guided to the right toy for his daughter and, after accessing reviews, can feel confident about his choice.

Buy and give the toy.

Spend time together playing. With access to many ideas for how to play, he can spend time with his daughter creating memories and growing their relationship. And the increased engagement with Treasure Chest online ensures the social shopping experience that is so important to Jason by building a relationship and bringing users back to the website to review their purchases.
